## Interval Legend
- **R** = Root / Unison
- **m2** = Minor 2nd (half step)
- **M2** = Major 2nd (whole step)
- **A2** = Augmented 2nd (same pitch as m3)
- **m3** = Minor 3rd
- **M3** = Major 3rd
- **d4** = Diminished 4th (same pitch as M3)
- **P4** = Perfect 4th
- **A4** = Augmented 4th / Tritone
- **d5** = Diminished 5th / Tritone
- **P5** = Perfect 5th
- **A5** = Augmented 5th (same pitch as m6)
- **m6** = Minor 6th
- **M6** = Major 6th
- **m7** = Minor 7th
- **M7** = Major 7th

## Key Differences from Church Modes
The harmonic minor scale and its modes feature several distinctive interval characteristics:

1. **Augmented 2nd (A2)**: Found in several modes (especially Phrygian Dominant and Lydian #2), this is the characteristic "exotic" interval of harmonic minor
2. **Augmented 5th (A5)**: Present in Harmonic Minor and Ionian #5, creating tension and expanding harmonic possibilities
3. **Diminished 4th (d4)**: Found in Super Locrian, creating unusual melodic motion
4. **More Augmented Intervals**: The raised 7th degree creates more augmented and diminished intervals throughout the system

## Notes
- Each scale degree shows the intervals available from that note to other notes within the same mode
- The harmonic minor system is particularly rich in augmented and diminished intervals due to the characteristic M7
- These modes are fundamental in jazz, flamenco, Middle Eastern music, and classical harmony
- The Aug5/m6 column shows the enharmonic relationship between these intervals
- Phrygian Dominant (Mode V) is extremely common in flamenco and klezmer music
- Super Locrian (Mode VII) is identical to the altered scale used extensively in jazz over dominant 7th chords
